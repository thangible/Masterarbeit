

\subsection{Input Adapter}


The ClimateInputAdapter is a specialized learnable preprocessing module designed to bridge the structural gap between multi-parameter atmospheric grids and the fixed input requirements of SAM. Since SAM is natively optimized for three-channel RGB imagery, this adapter compresses the 16 atmospheric variables from the ClimateNet dataset into a pseudo-RGB representation that preserves critical geophysical features.

\subsubsection{Nonlinear Convolutional Adapter}
As depicted in Figure \ref{fig:input_adapter_nonlinear}, the architecture follows a bottleneck convolutional design. It initiates with a 3x3 Conv2d layer to capture local spatial dependencies, followed by sequential 1x1 convolutions that reduce the feature dimensionality from 32 to 16, and ultimately to 3 channels. The module incorporates BatchNorm2d and ReLU activations for internal stabilization. A final Sigmoid activation function constrains the output values to a [0, 1] range, which are subsequently rescaled by 255.0 to align with the standard pixel intensity expected by the SAM image encoder.

To maintain physical grounding during the Adaptation Phase, the adapter employs an expert-knowledge initialization strategy. The weights for the most diagnostic variables TMQ, U850, V850 \cite{radke2024explaining} are set to 1.0 at the center of the convolution kernels. 

\subsubsection{Linear Adapter}

\begin{figure}[!h]
    \centering
    % \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{graphics/approach/prompt_pipeline-1.pdf}
    \makebox[\textwidth][c]{%
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{graphics/approach/input_adapter_linear.pdf}
}
\vspace{-1em}
    \caption{Linear Input Adapter Architecture}
    \label{fig:input_adapter_linear}
\end{figure}

After experimenting with the convolutional architecture, we observed that the performance of AR is significantly hindered by the spatial smoothing effect. To address this, we implemented a simpler Linear Adapter that directly projects the 16-channel input to 3 channels using a 2D convolution with a 1x1 kernel.  Similar to the nonlinear version, the weights corresponding to TMQ, U850, and V850 are initialized to 1.0, while the rest are initialized to zero, including the bias. The forward pass of this linear adapter can be mathematically expressed as:

\begin{equation}
Output_{n, i, j} = \sigma \left( \sum_{k=1}^{C_{in}} Input_{k, i, j} \cdot W_{n, k} + B_n \right)
\end{equation}

where $(i, j)$ represents the spatial coordinates, $k$ denotes the input channel index, and $B_{n}$ is the bias term for the $n$-th filter. Under this initialization, the weight matrix $W$ acts as a selection operator. For the three output channels intended to represent the RGB space, the initial state of the adapter simplifies to:

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
Output_{1, i, j} (R) &= Input_{TMQ, i, j} \\
Output_{2, i, j} (G) &= Input_{U850, i, j} \\
Output_{3, i, j} (B) &= Input_{V850, i, j}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
This configuration ensures that at the onset of training, the Red, Green, and Blue channels are directly mapped to the TMQ, U850, and V850 variables, respectively. The model can then learn to adjust these weights during training to optimize the representation for both TCs and ARs.

\subsubsection{Learnable Token-based approach}

Inspired by CAT-SAM and HQ-SAM \cite{xiao2024cat, ke2023segment}, we implement a learnable token-based adaptation strategy to enable SAM to effectively process complex multi-parameter atmospheric data. This approach involves introducing learnable tokens designed to capture the unique spatial and morphological characteristics of TCs and ARs. We explore two architectural variants: a Single Token Architecture and a Dual Token Architecture. The learnable tokens are integrated into the ViT encoder of SAM, allowing the model to learn class-specific localization priors and structural discovery independently for each meteorological feature.

% \vspace{-1em}
\begin{figure}[!h]
    \centering
    % \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{graphics/approach/prompt_pipeline-1.pdf}
    \makebox[0.9\textwidth][c]{%
    \includegraphics[width=1.1\textwidth]{graphics/approach/two_tokens.pdf}
}
\vspace{-1em}
    \caption{Dual Token Architecture}
    \label{fig:dual_token_architecture}
\end{figure}

The distinct geometric and spatial patterns exhibited by TCs and ARs motivate our design choices. TCs are characterized by circular structures, while ARs manifest as narrow, ribbon-like corridors. Single Token Architecture: As illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:single_token_architecture}, a single learnable token and MLP are shared across both classes. This variant serves as a baseline for unified feature representation. Dual Token Architecture: As depicted in Figure \ref{fig:dual_token_architecture}, separate tokens are dedicated to TCs and ARs, respectively. This allows the model to specialize its attention for each phenomenon.

Within the Dual Token Architecture, we experiment with two specific approaches for merging task-specific information. \textbf{Infused Token (Element-wise Addition)}: In the initial configuration, the two tokens are infused by averaging them via element-wise addition. However, during experimentation, we observed signs of catastrophic forgetting, where the model struggled to maintain distinct structural priors for both classes simultaneously as they competed for the same representational space. \textbf{Concat Token (Concatenation)}: To address this limitation, we transitioned to a concatenation strategy. In this variant, each adapter block outputs features with only half the dimension of the required prompt dimension. These are then concatenated to form a Merged Token. This design choice ensures that the spatial characteristics of TCs and ARs are preserved in separate subspaces, effectively mitigating feature interference and improving segmentation performance on the ClimateNet dataset.

The adapter mechanism is built around task-specific projection layers. We initialize two sets of sequential adapter modules, Adapter AR and Adapter TC, corresponding to the total number of layers in the SAM image encoder blocks. Each individual adapter block follows a bottleneck-style structure, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:dual_token_architecture}. It consists of a Linear layer that projects from the token dimension (256) to a latent space $F$, followed by a GELU activation function and a final Linear layer that projects to the prompt dimension required by the ViT layers. The complexity of these projections is controlled by an \textbf{mlp\_ratio} (which we will experiment with 0.5, 0.75 and 1), which determines the width of the intermediate latent dimension.  For every layer in the encoder, the dedicated AR and TC tokens are passed through their respective projection sequences to generate layer-specific updated tokens. These tokens are then merged (either via element-wise addition or concatenation) to form the final prompt that is fed into the ViT layers.

\subsubsection{LoRA approach}
To adapt SAM to interpret multi-parameter atmospheric grids, we apply LoRA adapters to each layer of the Vision Transformer (ViT) encoder. This "surgeon" approach specifically targets the Query ($q$), Key ($k$), and Value ($v$) projection layers within the multi-head self-attention mechanism.
\begin{figure}[!h]
    \centering
    % \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{graphics/approach/prompt_pipeline-1.pdf}
    \makebox[0.9\textwidth][c]{%
    \includegraphics[width=1.1\textwidth]{graphics/approach/lora_full.pdf}
}
\vspace{-1em}
    \caption{SAM with LoRA Architecture}
    \label{fig:lora_architecture}
\end{figure}

The core principle of LoRA is to constrain the weight updates of a pre-trained layer by representing them as a product of two low-rank matrices. For a frozen pre-trained weight matrix $W_{0} \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ and an input feature $x \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$, the adapted forward pass is expressed as:
\begin{equation}
h = W_{0}x + \Delta Wx = W_{0}x + \frac{\alpha}{r}BAx
\end{equation}
where $B \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}$ and $A \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times d}$ are the learnable low-rank matrices with rank $r \ll d$. We initialize matrix $A$ using a Kaiming uniform distribution and matrix $B$ to zero, ensuring that the initial adaptation state is identical to the original SAM. A scaling factor $\frac{\alpha}{r}$ is applied to stabilize training when varying the rank $r$.

As illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:lora_architecture}, the LoRA layers are integrated into the encoder adapter block. While a standard LoRA implementation uses a single set of adapters, we implement a specialized Dual LoRA architecture to address the distinct morphological characteristics of TCs and ARs. We name the first variant \textbf{Single LoRA} and the second variant \textbf{Dual LoRA}.

In the Dual LoRA configuration, the $qkv$ projection is modified to include separate adapter branches for each meteorological task: TC Adapters: Low-rank matrices $A_{tc}$ and $B_{tc}$ are optimized for the distinct circular structures of Tropical Cyclones. AR Adapters: Low-rank matrices $A_{ar}$ and $B_{ar}$ are tailored for the narrow, ribbon-like corridors of Atmospheric Rivers.

During the forward pass, a task mode flag controls which adapters are active. This allows the frozen SAM backbone to produce task-specialized embeddings ($encoder\_image\_tc$ and $encoder\_image\_ar$) by simply switching the active low-rank path. These specialized embeddings are then fed into independent mask decoder heads (Decoder\_TC and Decoder\_AR). This dual-decoder and dual-adapter design allows the model to learn class-specific localization priors and structural discovery independently for each feature, effectively managing the significant class imbalances inherent in climate data.

\subsection{Automatic Prompt Generator}

\subsubsection{CGNet}
We integrate CGNet as the auxiliary model for prompt generation for SAM pipeline. The implementation uses pretrained weights from the CG-Net model trained on the ClimateNet dataset, which are accessible via the official portal \footnote{\url{https://portal.nersc.gov/project/ClimateNet/climatenet_new/model/weights.pth}}. Figure \ref{fig:cg_net_architecture} illustrates the architecture of CGNet.

\begin{figure}[!h]
    \centering
    % \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{graphics/approach/prompt_pipeline-1.pdf}
    \makebox[0.9\textwidth][c]{%
    \includegraphics[width=1.1\textwidth]{graphics/approach/cg_net.png}
}
\vspace{-1em}
    \caption{CGNet Architecture \cite{wu2020cgnet}, Figure from Radke et al. \cite{radke2024explaining}}
    \label{fig:cg_net_architecture}
\end{figure}


CGNet is a Context Guided Network specifically designed for semantic segmentation with high computational efficiency. , This specific model contains only 494,232 parameters \cite{kapp2020spatio}. The architecture is built upon the CGBlock, which follows a joint feature extraction and refinement strategy. Figure \ref{fig:cg_block_architecture} illustrates the architecture of the CGBlock. The block first captures spatial dependencies by concatenating the outputs of a standard $3 \times 3$ convolution with a $3 \times 3$ dilated convolution. This allows the model to perceive both fine-grained local details and larger atmospheric structures simultaneously. Following concatenation, the representation is refined through a Global Context (FGlo) module. This module uses average pooling and a multi-layer-perceptron (MLP) to scale channels based on global image importance. To maintain the physical topology of global climate data—where the eastern and western edges of the raster are geographically continuous—the implementation utilizes circular padding instead of standard zero-padding.

\subsubsection{Multi-scale Fusion}
Self-Prompt SAM in area of 3D medical imaging proposed a multi-scale prompt generator (MSPGenerator) combined with the image encoder that employs the Vision Transformer (ViT) architecture \cite{xie2025self}. The MSPGenerator is a hierarchical structure built by 3D convolutional and transpose convolution layers. Starting with the deepest feature map, it is gradually upsampled to 2x the size and then concatenates with shallower feature maps.

Because the implementation of the original MSPGenerator is not explicitly described in the paper, we design our own version of the module based inspired the general description and the figure provided in the paper. 
 Figure \ref{fig:multiscalefusion} illustrated our implementation With limited computational resources, we replace the Convolutional transpose layers with bilinear upsampling, and we group the features maps in to groups of three then concat them together then fused. We still keepot he hierachical structure by gradually upsampling the feature maps and fusing them with shallower features. At the end of every fusion block of a group, we apply a convolutional layer to get the intermediate output for the loss function.
